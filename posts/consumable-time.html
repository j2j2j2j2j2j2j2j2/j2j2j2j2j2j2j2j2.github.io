The Missing Variable in AI Economics: Consumable Time

CitriniResearch's Ghost GDP scenario is sharp — but it omits the most powerful variable in every prior technological revolution.

CitriniResearch's "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" is one of the more intellectually honest AI bear cases I've read. It doesn't rely on sci-fi doom or regulatory collapse. It simply models a scenario where AI succeeds — productivity booms, corporate margins expand, GDP looks great — and yet the economy quietly hollows out from underneath.

The central concept, Ghost GDP, is genuinely clever: output that shows up in national accounts but never circulates through the real economy, because the machines generating it don't spend money on rent, food, or Netflix.

The scenario is worth taking seriously. But I think it's missing a variable that has been the decisive factor in every prior technological disruption: consumable time.

The Framework CitriniResearch Skips

Economic history has a consistent structure that the Ghost GDP scenario doesn't account for. Every major productivity leap — the plow, the steam engine, the assembly line, the personal computer — did two things simultaneously:

First, it destroyed existing jobs. Brutally and irreversibly. There's no going back to hand-weaving textiles once the Spinning Jenny exists. The CitriniResearch scenario is correct that AI will eliminate white-collar knowledge work at scale, and that this process is already underway.

But second — and this is the part the model misses — it freed human time. And freed human time has always found somewhere to go.

Consider the arc of agricultural productivity. For most of human history, the majority of the population spent the majority of their waking hours on food production. There was simply no spare time to do much else. Surplus agricultural labor, freed by better tools and seeds, didn't just sit idle — it flowed into trade, crafts, construction, religion, philosophy, and eventually into the early forms of what we'd recognize as an economy.

The Industrial Revolution did the same thing at scale. It didn't just create factories — it created the weekend, the concept of leisure, and eventually entire industries built around filling that leisure time. Cinema. Professional sports. Tourism. The department store. None of these were predictable from inside the pre-industrial economy. They emerged from the accumulated surplus of freed human time.

Ghost GDP vs. Ghost Time

The CitriniResearch model identifies Ghost GDP — economic output that doesn't circulate. But there's an inverse concept worth naming: Ghost Time — human hours that currently disappear into low-value, AI-replaceable knowledge work, which will be freed and available for other uses.

A mid-level analyst spending 60% of their week pulling data, formatting reports, and summarizing information isn't generating much economic surplus beyond their salary. When AI absorbs those tasks, something interesting happens: that person now has 25 hours a week that didn't meaningfully exist before.

What do humans do with freed time? History suggests they don't just stop spending. They find new ways to spend — both money and time. The question is whether new consumption categories emerge fast enough to absorb the transition.

The Speed Problem Is Real

I want to be careful not to dismiss the CitriniResearch scenario with historical optimism. There is a genuine risk that's different this time: velocity.

Previous technological disruptions gave societies one to three generations to adapt. The steam engine took roughly 50 years to fully transform the labor market. Electrification took 40. The personal computer, roughly 20-25. AI — specifically agentic AI — appears to be compressing this transition to a decade or less.

The Ghost GDP scenario is most dangerous not in its endpoint, but in its transition period. If job displacement happens at 10x the historical rate while new consumption categories emerge at historical rates, you get a gap. That gap is what the CitriniResearch memo is really modeling.

They're probably right that this gap will be painful. They may be wrong that it's permanent.

What the New Consumption Categories Look Like

The honest answer is: we don't know. This is structurally unknowable from inside the pre-transition economy.

In 1900, no one could have predicted that by 2000:

  • People would pay strangers to bring food to their door (food delivery)
  • Watching other people play video games would be a multi-billion dollar industry
  • Sharing photographs of meals would be a career
  • Owning a tiny computer that streams all of human music would cost $10/month

All of these emerged from the same basic dynamic: productivity gains freed time and purchasing power, humans searched for ways to fill that time, and new industries crystallized around what they found.

AI will likely do the same. The categories are opaque to us now. But some candidates are visible at the edges: hyper-personalized experiences that require human judgment to curate; physical experiences that can't be digitized; care work for aging populations; creative collaboration that uses AI as a tool rather than a replacement; new forms of community and meaning-making that emerge in response to AI mediation of so much else.

The Real Question CitriniResearch Should Have Asked

The memo frames its central question as: what if AI bullishness is right, and that's actually bearish?

The more important question is: how long is the gap, and who survives it?

Societies and individuals positioned closest to new consumption categories — or with the flexibility to participate in them as they emerge — will be fine. Those locked into the specific job categories AI replaces, with limited ability to adapt, will face genuine hardship during the transition window.

This is not a refutation of CitriniResearch's scenario. It's a specification of it. The crisis they model is real — but it's a transition crisis, not a terminal one. Ghost GDP is a phase, not a permanent state.

"The velocity of money flatlined. The human-centric consumer economy, 70% of GDP at the time, withered." — CitriniResearch, The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis

They're right that this could happen. But human economies have been 100% of GDP for the entirety of human history. That's not a coincidence or a fragile condition — it's what happens when you have a species that converts freed time into new desires at scale.

The machines don't buy things. But the humans who build, manage, direct, and are served by the machines still do. And the humans freed from the work the machines replaced — they'll find something to do with their time. They always have.
